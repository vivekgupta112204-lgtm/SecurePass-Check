Difference Between Passwords and Passphrases: Why Longer is Better

By SecurePass Team • 5 min read

The traditional password advice was to create complex gibberish. The modern advice is to create a "Passphrase"â€”a sentence or collection of words. Why the shift?

The Problem with Complexity

Humans are bad at randomness. When asked to create a complex password, we make predictable choices:

  • Capitalizing the first letter.
  • Adding a "1" or "!" at the end.
  • Replacing "a" with "@".

A computer can guess these patterns very quickly. Furthermore, strings like J8#kL2!m are impossible to remember, forcing users to write them down (unsafe) or reset them constantly (annoying).

Enter the Passphrase

A passphrase is a sequence of random words. For example:

purple-bicycle-eating-pizza

Why is this stronger?

  1. Length: This phrase is 27 characters long. The math of entropy heavily favors length. Even without numbers or symbols, a 27-character string is virtually unbreakable by brute force.
  2. Memorability: It is easy for a human brain to visualize a purple bicycle eating pizza. It is hard to visualize J8#kL2!m.
  3. Typing Speed: It is much faster to type words you know than to hunt for special symbols on your keyboard.

How to Build a Good Passphrase

Do:

  • Use 4 or more random, unrelated words.
  • Separate them with spaces or dashes.
  • Add one capital letter or number if the website requires it (e.g., Purple-bicycle-eating-pizza1).

Don't:

  • Use common sayings or lyrics (e.g., "To be or not to be"). Hackers have lists of these.
  • Use names of family members or pets.

The XKCD Method

This concept was famously illustrated by the webcomic XKCD, which showed that correcthorsebatterystaple took 550 years to crack, while Tr0ub4dor&3 took 3 days. The logic holds up: mathematical difficulty for computers, ease of memory for humans.
