Password Strength Explained: How Hackers Crack Weak Passwords

By SecurePass Team • 9 min read

To defeat your enemy, you must understand them. Hackers do not sit at a keyboard guessing "God" or "Love" manually. They use sophisticated automated tools. Here is a breakdown of the three most common attack methods.

1. Dictionary Attacks

This is the fastest and most common method. The hacker feeds a list of words (a "dictionary") into a program. This isn't just the Oxford English Dictionary; it includes common passwords like "password", "qwerty", "yankees", and "superman".

The Defense: Never use a standalone English word. "Dragon" is cracked in 0.0001 seconds. "Dr@g0n" is cracked in 0.5 seconds because the dictionary software also checks for common letter substitutions (Leet Speak).

2. Brute Force Attacks

If the dictionary attack fails, the software moves to Brute Force. This is checking every possible combination of characters: aaaa, aaab, aaac... until it finds the match.

The Math: A 7-character password has about 3 trillion combinations. A modern GPU cluster can check billions of passwords per second. This means short passwords fall in minutes. However, a 12-character password has quadrillions of combinations, taking centuries to crack.

The Defense: Length. Length. Length. Every character you add increases the difficulty exponentially.

3. Rainbow Tables

When websites store your password, they (hopefully) store it as a "Hash"â€”a scrambled string of text. A Rainbow Table is a pre-computed list of millions of passwords and their corresponding hash values. Hackers can simply look up the hash of your password in this giant table to find the original text instantly.

The Defense: This is mostly up to the website developer (who should use "Salted" hashes), but using a unique, long password ensures it won't be in the hacker's pre-computed table.

4. Social Engineering & Phishing

Sometimes, the easiest way to break a password is to ask for it. Phishing emails that look like they come from "Google Support" or "Netflix Billing" trick you into typing your password into a fake website.

The Defense: Always check the URL bar. Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) so that even if they get your password, they cannot login.

Summary

Hackers are lazy; they look for low-hanging fruit. By ensuring your password is long (12+ characters), unique (not reused), and random (high entropy), you make yourself too difficult a target to bother with.
