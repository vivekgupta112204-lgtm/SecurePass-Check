The Mathematics of Safety: Understanding Password Entropy

SP
SecurePass Analysis Team
• 12 min read • Updated 2026

Why is a 20-character phrase of simple words stronger than an 8-character string of gibberish? The answer lies in a concept borrowed from thermodynamics and information theory: Entropy.

When most people think of a "strong" password, they imagine a chaotic jumble of letters, numbers, and symbols like T&9#kL!x. While this is certainly better than "password123," it relies on the user's ability to memorize abstract data. However, cybersecurity experts and mathematicians look at passwords differently. We don't just look at "complexity"; we look at Information Entropy.

This article will explain the math behind password security, stripping away the jargon to reveal exactly how hackers crack codes—and how you can make yours mathematically unbreakable.

What is Password Entropy?

In cybersecurity, entropy is a measurement of how unpredictable a password is. It is measured in bits. The higher the number of bits, the harder it is for a computer to guess the password using brute-force methods.

Think of it like a coin toss:

  • A coin has 2 possibilities (Heads or Tails). One toss has 1 bit of entropy.
  • If you toss it twice, there are 4 possibilities (HH, HT, TH, TT). That is 2 bits of entropy.
  • If you toss it 10 times, there are 1,024 possibilities. That is 10 bits of entropy.

With passwords, we aren't using coins; we are using characters. The "Pool" of possibilities is much larger than 2, but the math is the same. Every character you add multiplies the total number of possibilities, increasing the entropy.

The Formula for Strength

For the math enthusiasts, the entropy ($E$) of a password can be calculated using this formula:

E = log₂(Rᴸ)

Where:

  • R (Pool Size): The number of unique characters available (e.g., 26 for lowercase letters).
  • L (Length): The number of characters in your password.

This formula reveals a crucial secret: Length (L) is an exponent. Increasing the pool size (R) helps linearly, but increasing the length (L) helps exponentially.

The "Pool Size" Explained

When a hacker's software attempts to crack your password, it needs to know which characters to try. This determines the size of the "Pool."

Character Set Pool Size (R) Examples
Numbers Only 10 0-9
Lowercase Only 26 a-z
Alphanumeric 62 a-z, A-Z, 0-9
Full Keyboard 95 All symbols (~!@#...)

Most users think adding a "!" at the end of their password makes it secure. While it increases the pool size from 62 to 95, it only adds a small amount of mathematical difficulty compared to adding more characters.

Why Length Beats Complexity

Let's run the numbers on two different passwords. Assume a hacker can make 100 billion guesses per second (a capability available to advanced GPU clusters today).

Password A: "Tr0ub4!" (Complex, Short)

  • Length: 7 characters
  • Pool: 95 (Full Keyboard)
  • Combinations: ~70 trillion
  • Time to Crack: 11 minutes

Password B: "horse-staple-battery" (Simple, Long)

  • Length: 20 characters
  • Pool: 27 (Lowercase + Hyphen)
  • Combinations: ~40 octillion
  • Time to Crack: 12 billion years

Even though "Password B" uses only simple lowercase letters, the sheer exponent of its length makes it mathematically impossible to crack by brute force in the lifetime of the universe. This is why we advocate for Passphrases over complex passwords.

The "Human Factor" in Entropy

Mathematics assumes randomness. However, humans are notoriously bad at being random. If you choose a password like Password123!, the mathematical entropy might look decent on paper because it has uppercase, lowercase, numbers, and symbols.

However, hackers know human psychology. They know you likely capitalized the first letter. They know you likely put the number at the end. They use "Dictionary Attacks" that skip the brute force math entirely and guess common patterns first.

True Entropy requires unpredictability. This is why random word generators (or dice rolls) are superior to sentences you make up yourself.

How Many Bits of Entropy Do You Need?

In 2026, the standards for security have evolved. Here is a general guide to entropy bits:

  • < 40 Bits (Weak): Can be cracked instantly or within minutes. Avoid for anything.
  • 40-60 Bits (Moderate): Acceptable for low-risk accounts (like a news site login), but vulnerable to dedicated attacks.
  • 60-80 Bits (Strong): Safe for most standard users. Would take months or years to brute force.
  • > 80 Bits (Excellent): Resistant to nation-state level attacks. Recommended for banking, email, and password managers.
  • > 100 Bits (Paranoid): Mathematically unbreakable with foreseeable technology.

Practical Steps to Increase Entropy

You don't need to be a mathematician to secure your accounts. Follow these three rules to maximize your entropy:

  1. Abandon the 8-Character Habit: Most people stick to the minimum required length. Always exceed it. A 15-character password is the new baseline for safety.
  2. Use 4+ Random Words: String together four random words. correct-horse-battery-staple is a famous example (though don't use that specific one!).
  3. Let the Machines Do It: The best way to get high entropy is to use a Password Manager. These tools generate cryptographically random strings like 8#xP2$mQ9vZL which have maximum entropy density.

Key Takeaways

  • Entropy measures unpredictability. It is the true metric of password strength, not just "complexity."
  • Length is your best friend. Adding characters increases security exponentially.
  • Humans are predictable. Use random word generators or password managers to avoid common patterns.
  • Aim for 60+ bits. This usually means a password of at least 12 random characters or a passphrase of 4 words.

Frequently Asked Questions

Does adding a number at the end increase entropy?

Technically, yes, but only slightly. Because it is a predictable pattern (humans almost always put numbers at the end), sophisticated cracking tools anticipate this. It is better to place numbers in the middle or rely on longer passphrases.

Can a computer crack a 20-character password?

If the password is truly random (high entropy), it is virtually impossible with current technology. However, if the 20 characters are a famous quote or song lyric, it has very low entropy and can be cracked easily via a dictionary attack.

Is a fingerprint safer than a password?

Biometrics (fingerprints, FaceID) serve a different purpose. They are usernames, not secrets (you leave your fingerprints on everything you touch). They are convenient, but a high-entropy password is still the ultimate fallback for security.
